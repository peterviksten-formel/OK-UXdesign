# The Four Lenses

Four mental moves borrowed from practitioners outside web design. They are not templates. They are *questions to ask a brand* before suggesting any execution. Pick the lens that fits the surface and the brand's actual point of view.

## Lens 1 — Rory Sutherland: Reframe the problem

Sutherland, Vice Chairman of Ogilvy, built his career on a single conviction: organizations systemically bias toward engineering solutions when psychological solutions are almost always cheaper, more effective, and more interesting. His most famous illustration: the proposal to spend six billion pounds on new rail infrastructure to cut the London–Edinburgh journey by forty minutes. His counter-proposal: hire supermodels to serve free Château Pétrus for the duration of the ride. Cost: a fraction. Outcome: passengers asking for the trains to be slowed down.

**The principle:** *Value is not in the object; it is in the perception of the object.*

**The mental move:** Solve for the feeling first, then reverse-engineer the design that produces it.

**The question to ask a brand:** Before you accept the brief as given, is the problem correctly framed? A checkout flow that "feels anxious" is not fixed by making it faster — it might be fixed by adding one warmly-worded reassurance at the moment the anxiety spikes. A pricing page that converts poorly is not necessarily a layout problem — the framing of value may be wrong. Is the real problem what the brief says it is?

**When this lens fits:**
- Moments where the user is anxious, impatient, or unsure (checkout, account deletion, payment failure, long wait)
- Any surface where the team's instinct is to "make it faster" or "make it cleaner" — and you suspect that's the wrong axis
- Situations where a single well-placed piece of reassurance or context would change the whole feeling

**When this lens doesn't fit:**
- Surfaces where the user's feeling is already well-matched to the interface (a confident CTA on a delight-filled landing page)
- Pure information-density moments where reframing would add cognitive load

**Example prompt to offer the user:** "What is the user feeling at this moment — and is that feeling actually fixed by the design change you were considering, or is it fixed by something else entirely?"

## Lens 2 — Erik Kessels: Make the embarrassment the brand

Kessels, co-founder of KesselsKramer in Amsterdam, wrote *Failed It!* as a manifesto arguing that mistakes are the only honest path to originality. His most instructive work is the fifteen-year campaign for the Hans Brinker Budget Hotel — an Amsterdam hostel positioned, explicitly, as the worst hotel in the world. Ads: "Now complimentary toilet flushing." Tiny promotional flags placed in piles of dog excrement around the city. The hotel ran at maximum occupancy. Guests who arrived expecting the worst were delighted to find it merely bad.

**The principle:** *Perfection is the worst starting point. The relentless pursuit of polish is the enemy of personality.*

**The mental move:** Find the thing the brand is embarrassed about — then make it the brand.

**The question to ask a brand:** What are you hiding or smoothing over that, if you owned it, would build more trust than your polish is currently buying you? What's the "honest thing" at this surface that the interface is currently pretending isn't true?

**When this lens fits:**
- Brands with a genuine point of view already; brands aware of their own limits
- Categories where consumers are skeptical of polished marketing (cheap hostels, budget airlines, D2C food, anything "anti-corporate")
- Moments where honesty *about* the experience would reframe the experience (a loading screen that admits it's making you wait; an onboarding that says "this next part is kind of tedious but worth it")

**When this lens doesn't fit:**
- Brands that genuinely are premium/luxury — owning imperfection here reads as failed confidence, not honest charm
- Moments where the user is looking for trust and reassurance (payment confirmation, security warning)
- Categories where safety/reliability *is* the product (medical, financial core surfaces)

**The warning:** This lens gets misused constantly. "Be honest" is not permission to ship bad work — it's permission to name the gap between ambition and reality *where that gap is the brand's truth*. Don't invert polish into embarrassment for its own sake.

**Example prompt to offer the user:** "What's one true thing about this product that your current interface is pretending isn't true? What would happen if this surface said it?"

## Lens 3 — MSCHF: Concept before component

MSCHF, the Brooklyn-based art collective, describes its drops as "performance art using consumer culture, mass media, and celebrity as artistic mediums." They operate on a logic most brand teams have never considered: the visual identity, the product, the channel, and the concept are the same thing. They do not design a product and then brand it. The concept generates everything — including whether there's a product at all.

For a Fenty Beauty collaboration, MSCHF produced six ketchup packets: some contained Fenty lip gloss, some contained ketchup. No way to know which until you opened it. The "design" was not visual. It was structural. The experience *was* the statement.

MSCHF's most radical principle: no preexisting visual identity. Each project gets a new identity, built from scratch, to serve that specific concept. Consistency comes from consistency of intent, not from a logo system.

**The principle:** *The concept generates everything. Every element must justify its existence with an idea, not just a function.*

**The mental move:** Start from the concept, not the component. What is this page actually *saying*, before you decide how it looks?

**The question to ask a brand:** If this surface were a statement instead of an element — a sentence rather than a slot — what would it be saying? Can the surface itself *be* the idea, rather than a container for one?

**When this lens fits:**
- Surfaces that are usually treated as afterthoughts (404, empty states, maintenance pages) — these are where you have the most room to let the concept lead
- Brands with a willingness to be unmistakable rather than safe
- Moments where the user has a second to look and absorb rather than pass through quickly (the footer on a content-heavy site; a landing page hero)
- Campaigns, launches, or moments where the whole experience can be concept-led rather than template-led

**When this lens doesn't fit:**
- Core transactional flows where getting-the-job-done outranks being-unmistakable
- Brands whose value proposition is calm, quiet, and professional — concept-first work here reads as showing off
- Any surface where the user is mid-task and concept-led work would interrupt flow

**The warning:** Concept-first is not "be weird." It's "know what you're saying before you decide how to say it." Weirdness without idea is just noise.

**Example prompt to offer the user:** "If this page/surface had to defend its existence with a one-sentence idea, what would that sentence be? Is the current design actually saying that sentence, or is it saying 'we made a 404 page because every site needs one'?"

## Lens 4 — Virgil Abloh: The 3% rule and show the thinking

Abloh's 3% rule: *change something by just three per cent, and its meaning transforms completely.* Creativity is not invention — it is calibration. The talent is identifying the precise intervention that shifts an existing thing into something new. "Design is like DJing. You take existing sounds and recontextualise them."

The quotation marks across Off-White products — shoelaces labelled "Shoelaces," bags labelled "Sculpture," boots labelled "For Walking" — are a direct application. Two characters of punctuation. Redesign judo. The quotation marks don't describe the object — they question it. They make the design process visible.

Abloh's second principle, running through all his work: *transparency is a design tool.* "The work needs to tell the story of how as an artist I have rationalized the world." Showing the thinking is part of the meaning.

**Two principles, used together:**
1. *Find the smallest intervention that changes the most.*
2. *Make the thinking visible.*

**The mental move:** What is the 3% that changes the whole? Then: can the brand let the user see it choosing?

**The question to ask a brand:** For any given element, what is the single smallest change that would shift how it reads? Not a redesign — a recontextualization. A label reframed as a question. A button that names itself. A hover state that reveals a thought rather than a color.

**When this lens fits:**
- Mature interfaces where a full redesign is off the table but small moves are welcome
- Moments where the existing element is almost right but feels lifeless
- Brands that can carry a slightly-self-aware tone without tipping into smug
- Secondary surfaces (footer, about, form hints) where a small move has big effect for low cost

**When this lens doesn't fit:**
- Early-stage design where the whole system hasn't been defined yet
- Brands whose truth is quiet seriousness — visible self-awareness undermines the register
- Situations where the 3% change would cost 300% of the engineering budget

**Example prompt to offer the user:** "What's one word, one punctuation mark, one micro-decision you could change on this surface that would make it read completely differently? And is there a way to let the user *see* you making that choice?"

## How to pick between lenses

| The surface is about… | Lean toward |
|---|---|
| A moment of anxiety, waiting, or uncertainty | Sutherland |
| A thing the brand is smoothing over or hiding | Kessels |
| An afterthought surface that deserves a bigger idea | MSCHF |
| An element that's almost-right and just needs calibration | Abloh |

And sometimes the right lens is none of them. That's a legitimate answer. *"I don't think any of these lenses applies cleanly here — the move I'd suggest is just more restraint"* is a better response than contorting a lens to fit.

## The deeper shared logic

All four thinkers perform the same prior move before any execution: they question whether the default answer is the right answer. Sutherland asks if the problem is correctly framed. Kessels asks if polish is serving or suppressing authenticity. MSCHF asks if the concept is strong enough to generate the design. Abloh asks what the minimum intervention is that produces maximum meaning.

This is the interrogation that has to happen *before* the skill suggests anything concrete. If you're generating suggestions without doing it, you're just producing sprinkles.
